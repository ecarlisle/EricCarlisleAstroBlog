An accessibility audit isn't a one-time quality gate — it's a diagnostic that tells you where your product fails for people with disabilities. The goal is a prioritized list of fixes, not a score.

## Automated Scanning

Tools like axe DevTools, WAVE, and Lighthouse catch roughly 30% of accessibility issues. They're excellent at finding missing alt text, insufficient color contrast, and missing form labels. Run them in CI alongside your linter.

But automated tools can't tell you if a focus order makes sense or if a screen reader announcement is confusing. That's where manual testing comes in.

## Manual Testing Checklist

1. Navigate the entire page with Tab and Shift+Tab. Is every interactive element reachable? Do you ever lose focus or get trapped?
2. Turn on a screen reader (VoiceOver on Mac, NVDA on Windows). Navigate by heading, by landmark, and by tab. Does the page structure make sense?
3. Zoom to 200%. Is everything still readable without horizontal scrolling?
4. Disable images. Is all essential information available as text alternatives?

## Prioritization

- **P0**: Blocks a critical user flow (can't submit a form with a screen reader)
- **P1**: Makes a flow significantly harder (poor focus order, unclear announcements)
- **P2**: Violates best practices but has a workaround (missing ARIA labels on decorative elements)

Fix P0 issues immediately. Schedule P1 for the next sprint. Track P2 in the backlog.

## Building an Accessibility Culture

The audit is only as good as the follow-through. Assign owners to each finding, link to the specific WCAG criterion, and verify the fix by running the failing check again. Accessibility is a habit, not a milestone.
