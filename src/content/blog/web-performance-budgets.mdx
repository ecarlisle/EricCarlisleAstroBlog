Performance budgets are the guardrails that keep a project from gradually slowing down. Without them, every new feature, library, or image adds a little more weight, and before you know it, your bundle has doubled.

This isn't about arbitrary limits. A good budget is derived from real data — your users' devices, network conditions, and business metrics.

## Choosing Your Metrics

Start with metrics you can measure consistently. Lighthouse scores are a good conversation starter, but for CI enforcement, use raw timing metrics:

- **LCP** should stay under 2.5s on the 75th percentile
- **Total bundle JS** under 150 KB gzipped for initial route
- **Total image weight** under 500 KB per page

These aren't universal. If your audience is mostly mobile users on 3G in India, your thresholds need to be tighter than if they're desktop users on fiber in North America.

## Tools for Enforcement

Lighthouse CI integrates into your CI pipeline and fails a build when a budget is exceeded. Bundle Buddy and Webpack Bundle Analyzer give you per-component breakdowns so you can see exactly which import pushed you over.

The key is to make the failure visible. A passing build that's 5 KB over budget is still over budget. Every regression needs a ticket.

## When to Relax a Budget

Budgets should be updated when you add a genuinely valuable feature, not when you're sloppy. The process: propose the increase, justify it with the feature's value, and look for something else to cut first.

This creates a culture of performance consciousness. Every team member thinks twice before adding that 50 KB dependency.

## TL;DR

Set thresholds based on real user data, enforce them in CI, and treat every overage as a conscious trade-off rather than an accident.
